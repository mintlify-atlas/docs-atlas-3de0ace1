---
title: "Flight Data Recorder Recovery and Chip-Off Process"
description: "How NTSB used chip-off extraction and the Xeltek SP-6100 reader to recover FDR data after a cracked FLASH chip rendered conventional downloads impossible."
---

The FDR storage module arrived at NTSB with visible cracking on FLASH chip U2, extending from the package edge through the center of the device. Conventional downloads via Playback32 and the DLDR chip-level tool returned all-zero data, confirming the on-board read path was broken. NTSB developed a chip-off extraction procedure — physically removing each FLASH chip from the board and reading it directly in an external programmer — to recover whatever data survived. Five of the six flight-data chips yielded readable data; U2's internal silicon was cracked beyond recovery.

## Recovery process

<Steps>
  <Step title="FDR circuit card assembly received at NTSB">
    The FDR circuit card assembly (CCA) was delivered to NTSB on April 4, 2022. The red RTV conformal coating protecting the storage board was intact, with only the FLASH temperature indicator point exposed. The temperature indicator showed the board had not experienced significant heat, and the connector damage observed on the CVR board was not present.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Playback32 download attempt — failed">
    NTSB configured a reference rack as a suitable FDR substitute chassis and connected the accident storage module. A Playback32 download was attempted. It did not succeed and produced no usable output.
  </Step>
  <Step title="DLDR chip-level download — all-zero results">
    Multiple DLDR chip-level downloads were attempted on individual FLASH chips. Results varied slightly between runs but consistently showed each chip image as entirely zero data — indicating the on-board read path was non-functional.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Visual and microscopic inspection — chip U2 crack identified">
    The RTV conformal coating was removed to enable a full visual inspection. Under high-magnification microscopy, chip U2 showed a crack running from the top edge of the package through the center of the device, penetrating the conformal coating on the device surface. Additional cracking was observed in the epoxy ball bonds securing U2 and several other chips to the board. Boards on both sides of the module showed widespread epoxy bond fractures.
  </Step>
  <Step title="X-ray inspection confirmed silicon-level cracking in U2">
    X-ray imaging was performed to assess damage not visible optically, including wire bond fractures and silicon die damage. The X-ray images confirmed that cracks in U2 passed through the silicon die region. Some cracks aligned with the copper X-frame substrate inside the package and were not visible in the X-ray, but the extent of the damage confirmed U2's data was not recoverable through any means. No additional unexpected damage was found in the other chips.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Chip-off procedure developed using Finetech FinePlacer and Xeltek SP-6100">
    To validate the chip-off method before working on the accident module, NTSB first performed the full procedure on a known-good reference board:

    1. DLDR chip images were captured from the reference board before chip removal for comparison.
    2. Conformal coating was removed from all chip pins. Epoxy bonds between chips were manually broken. Excess solder was removed by hand before using automated equipment.
    3. A removal profile was developed for the Finetech FinePlacer microelectronics station: heat the entire board to below solder melting point, apply focused heat to each chip via a dedicated nozzle at a controlled ramp rate to melting temperature, then lift and remove the chip by vacuum.
    4. Each removed chip was cleaned of residual solder and placed in the Xeltek SP-6100 FLASH reader/programmer socket for readout.

    Chip images from the Xeltek reader matched the DLDR images after correcting for byte-swap (DLDR produces big-endian; the Xeltek reader produces little-endian). Honeywell's CHIPS tool accepts both formats. A DLU file built from Xeltek images was functionally identical to one built from DLDR images, validating the chip-off procedure.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Chip-off performed on accident module — U1, U3–U6 read successfully">
    With CAAC approval, the same chip-off procedure was applied to the accident module. All six target chips (U1–U6) were removed successfully using the Finetech FinePlacer.

    Examination of U2 after removal revealed additional crack networks on the chip's underside beyond those visible before removal. Chips U1, U3, U4, U5, and U6 showed no additional damage after removal and were read successfully in the Xeltek SP-6100.

    U2 could not be read. The Xeltek reported numerous pin-check errors and indicated multiple pin shorts — consistent with severe internal silicon damage confirmed by subsequent X-ray and CT scanning of the removed chip.
  </Step>
  <Step title="U2 placeholder file (0xFF) used to reconstruct DLU">
    A placeholder binary file filled entirely with hexadecimal `FF` (all binary 1s) was created to substitute for the unreadable U2 chip image. This placeholder was used with Honeywell's CHIPS tool to assemble a complete FDR `.dlu` file from the five good chip images plus the placeholder.

    The assembled DLU was imported into NTSB's CIDER data analysis application. Using the Boeing-supplied data frame definition, CIDER de-framed the data. After de-framing, the accident flight data was located in the middle of the data stream (a result of missing U2 pointer data) and was manually repositioned to the end of the record.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Data imported to CIDER; approximately 150 parameters validated">
    Over 1,000 parameters are recorded in the Boeing 737-800 data frame. Approximately 150 were validated — confirmed to be correctly recorded with accurate engineering-unit scaling and offsets. Validation focused on:

    - Basic aircraft state: acceleration, position, altitude, attitude, airspeed
    - Flight control system, control surfaces, and control forces
    - Engine performance parameters

    The full validated parameter list is in Appendix B of the source report and documented in [FDR validated parameters](/data/fdr-parameters).
  </Step>
  <Step title="Manual time correction for final 12 minutes">
    Due to the missing U2 chip, the reconstructed data stream contains regular gaps wherever U2 data would have been written. Correcting the resulting sub-frame sync errors was highly labor-intensive (see data gap details below), so only the final 12 minutes of the accident flight received full manual time correction. A portion of the preceding landing and taxi was also corrected to assist parameter validation.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Data gap pattern

The HFR5-D interleaves flight data across all six storage chips in a striped pattern. At 512 words per second, each chip holds approximately 1.3 seconds of data per write cycle. The six chips together cover one full cycle of approximately 6.5 seconds before returning to chip U1.

Because U2's data is entirely missing, the reconstructed data stream has a gap of approximately **1.3 seconds every 6.5 seconds** throughout the recording.

## Sub-frame sync issue

The gaps from the missing U2 chip frequently span sub-frame boundaries. When the analysis software encountered the all-`FF` placeholder data, it lost synchronization and automatically inserted additional sub-frames to fill the gap. This resulted in approximately **4 extra sub-frames inserted for every 8 real sub-frames**, making the apparent data gaps roughly 4 seconds rather than the correct 1.3 seconds, and rendering the time axis inaccurate.

Correcting each gap required manually inspecting every sub-frame boundary in the data stream, identifying the start and end of each U2 gap, and inserting the correct missing sync boundaries so the playback software could maintain synchronization and display accurate time.

<Warning>
  FDR data ends at approximately 26,000 feet during the descent. The final portion of the descent and the impact sequence are not recorded. The FDR has no battery backup; it lost power when both engines' N2 speeds dropped below the generator disconnect threshold, cutting aircraft electrical power to the recorder.
</Warning>

<Note>
  During cruise at 29,000 feet, both engines' fuel cutoff switches were moved from the RUN position to the CUTOFF position. Engine N2 speeds subsequently dropped. When N2 fell below the generator disconnect threshold, the FDR lost power and stopped recording. The CVR, which has a battery backup providing at least 10 additional minutes of recording, continued capturing audio after the FDR stopped.
</Note>
